Financial Disclosure

A platform that simplifies the financial disclosure process for clients while promoting productive collaboration with other professionals.

Problem Statement

Financial disclosure is typically the most time consuming, expensive, and unpleasant process in the dispute resolution process.

Simplifying this processes on a professional platform helps minimize risks and complications while reducing expenses by 86%.

Project Duration

14 weeks
(May - Aug 2018)

Team Size

Design team of 2
(including me)

My Responsibilities

User Research, User Flows, Wireframes, Prototypes, Analysis

Tools Used

Office Suite, Balsamiq, Adobe Photoshop, Figma, Sketch, InVision

Like many things, the design squiggle can be used to understand the divorce process.

Defining the Problem Space

For research throughout the project, we collaborated with an industry partner who gave us access to information regarding their practice. Since we focused on dispute resolution, we looked into the legal separation process in Ontario. These were the key insights in our user discovery process.

Discovery

Secondary Research

Retting et al. suggests that in dispute processes such as in divorce proceedings, individuals are primarily concerned with fair procedures and procedural justice. Elements of procedural justice include exhibiting respect, voice, understanding, neutral decision-making, and helpfulness.

Procedural justice research has shown that when defendants and litigants perceive the court process to be fair, they are more likely to comply with court orders and to follow the law in the future, regardless of whether they “win” or “lose” their case.

As such, procedural justice was a concept that we constantly used when empathizing with users.

Needing a better understanding of the current divorce process, we collaborated with a local divorce firm and summarized their divorce process into six steps:

A financial disclosure (second step) is a holistic snapshot of a client's financial situation including assets, liabilities, expenses, etc. It typically involves the help of CDFAs, lawyers, and mediators. This step allows legal proceedings to divide financial components evenly among spouses in a divorce. Our research with the firm suggested that financial disclosure is the most painful step.

Competitive Heatmap Analysis

To understand the legal-tech climate as it pertained to dispute resolution, we analysed eight other competitive solutions.

We asked several legal professionals from our industry partner to rate each product on various features to formulate a heatmap (darker shades represent higher values).

There seemed to be a lack of affordable solutions that addressed financial disclosure document generation, professional collaboration, and client experience management.

Understanding the User

The core principle of user-centered design is involving the user within every part of the process. Developing empathy for our users as opposed to making assumptions allows us to determine the direction of our solution and to clarify what our users need throughout the process.

User Analysis

Representative Personas

We observed numerous phone calls and other interactions between the industry partner's professionals and their clients for a period of over two weeks (with the consent of both parties).

For more context, we interviewed the professionals regarding each client case to create personas.

Empathy Maps

Following the development of our two personas, we also used the observed interactions of the professionals and their clients to build empathy maps. This forces us to overcome personal biases and perceptions to map how our users actually felt during this process.

As for the client in particular, we specifically studied the Kübler-Ross Change Curve to understand the emotional impact of sudden, unwanted change (such as in the case of separation).

Analysing the Scope

In order to define the project scope, it is vital that we combine our understanding of the project scope and our empathy with our users. We used the personas we created in the previous step to define their needs, addressed these needs by ideating product features, and mapped the user flow.

Ideation

User Needs

By empathizing with our users, we were able to define their unique needs. In summary:

“

I need to gather client documents on a centralized platform so I don't have to worry about printing / courier expenses, as well as losing important documents.
I need to connect with other professionals so that I can collaborate and share client documents (with their consent) on an as-needed basis.
I need to monitor the progress of client's financial disclosures so I can offer help if they get stuck or when they need motivation.
I need to have legal forms properly filled based on the disclosure so that I don't need to waste the client's money and my time redundantly filling it myself.
Laura Mackenzie Family Lawyer

“

I need to convenient way to have my lawyer validate my documents so that I don't have to ship those documents to her, only to have it sent back.
I need to understand complicated legal and financial terms so that I can know exactly what to do at any given time without feeling overwhelmed.
I need to constantly see my own progress so that I can know exactly where I am and how much work is left before my disclosure is completed.​​
I need to gather old documents to prove my financial situation rather than going to banks and other places and collecting everything myself.
Charlie Hartridge Divorce Client

User Flows

While keeping legal and business constraints in mind, we translated these features into user flows. We structured these flows based on practitioners and clients, then subdivided the flows for new practitioners and returning practitioners.

We were sure to keep in mind the collaborative aspects of the platform, and how professionals would be able to send digital "handshakes" when they want to share a client file with another professional for more specialized aspects of completion.

For clients, we broke the process down into a screening questionnaire, followed by an ongoing checklist of the documents they needed to provide (in situations where the platform did not automatically source the documents through a secure API connection with a third party).

Designing the Solution

Now that we specified potential solutions and goals, we quickly developed prototypes to visualize and quantify the experience we were proposing. This also allowed us to explore the information architecture and the sequential process of each task in greater detail.

Prototyping

Wireframes

We specifically used the Fogg-Behavioral model and Hick's law for understanding how to increase client motivation for the completion of some more difficult tasks. We applied our understanding of these theories to develop wireframes for the platform and for particular interactions.

Lean Evaluation

We gathered feedback regarding the wireframes through client-walkthroughs and interviews with professionals from our industry partner. With their experience conducting financial disclosures, they had excellent feedback regarding our innovation and insight into how a client might feel.

What Went Well
Our participants liked the idea of simplifying the process to the client in steps and the way progress was always visualized in the interface. They especially liked that users had the option to do the screening interview themselves or with a professional leading them. Finally, they found that autofilling information from the disclosure into legal documents was extremely valuable.

What Could be Improved
Moving forward, we needed to address user's concerns about integrating a client management system into the platform for multiple disclosures, the client's ability to edit and see submitted documents, and the professional's ability to keep track of related activities by collaborators.

Verifying the Efficacy

Before the next design iteration, it's imperative that we conduct summative research to examine our progress and the effectiveness of our prototype. We used the DECIDE framework and elements of procedural justice to validate any of our previous assumptions and examine the efficacy of the MVP.

Evaluation

Focus Group Role-Play Study

We gathered five family law professionals from our industry partner as well as five participants who approximately fit the demographic of the client persona and had no experience with the financial disclosure process.

To introduce the platform to the focus group, we asked both persona types to role-play a professional-client relationship and complete a financial disclosure with data provided via an information package. This included navigating the dashboard, as well as completing a client interview, checklist, file validation, and automatic legal document generation.

After making observations and a brief feedback discussion, we also asked the participant to complete the System Usability Scale, a peer-reviewed and standardized measurement of usability where a score of 68 is considered average.

Our mean SUS score was 66, which was slightly below average. We believe our score was slightly lower than average given the complexity of our application; we used the data collected during evaluation to plan and prioritize a platform redesign.

Usability Analysis

An expert review of our platform was conducted using Nielsen's 10 usability heuristics.

After noting examples of heuristic support, we specified the source of specific usability issues.

Notably, we discovered that it was occasionally unclear what the user was supposed to do and some visual metaphors were unintuitive. Based on my previous research and given the typical age and emotional state of the clients, we decided to rely more on textual and explicit descriptions.

Platform Redesign

Using the data collected during the evaluation stage, we organized our revisions according to MoSCoW prioritization analysis and restructured our high-fidelity designs.

Iteration

Style Guide

Second Iteration

Using the feedback from our evaluation session, we worked to simplify the platform further and make the interface more intuitive.

One of our primary focuses was on redesigning the checklist portion - we organized the UI according to tasks that the client needs to do, tasks that the professional needs to do, and what has been completed. This highlights what the user needs to complete at any given time.

Lean evaluation with of the redesign showed that we improved the platform's SUS score to 71, where the average standardized SUS score is 68. Subsequent focus group discussions showed that the platform showed improvement in terms of the overall quality of experience as well.

Reflection

According to a cost evaluation based on competitive analyses, this platform can help the user save 86% of monetary expenses compared to the traditional process. By the end of this project, this platform also secured an official partnership with the Ontario Association for Family Mediation. Along the way, I learned three key things that will extend to my future work:

Keep it Quick, Keep it Agile

Many aspects of user-centered design seem linear; just because we learn about different tools in school doesn't mean we have to use them for every project! Weigh the pros and cons and ship a MVP for quick, iterative prototyping.

Cross-Collaboration is Key

Working in a small team allowed me to communicate with members beyond my scope of expertise. By learning more and more about their work, I was to understand their constraints and requirements to balance user needs with business needs.

Missed the Mark? Do it Again

We felt distraught when we learned that weeks of research and hard work led to a subpar usability score. With a month left to redesign the platform, we applied lean UX principles with quick iterations to find success.

Moving forward with this solution, we would like to analyse the platform longitudinally to verify that it saves time for our users compared to the traditional process. Additionally, we want to continue exploring deeper areas of brand customization to support buy-in from enterprise-level users and professionals who manage small teams on the platform.

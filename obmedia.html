Obsolete Media

A digital museum that preserves the memory of over six hundred current and obsolete media formats.

Project Brief

The Museum of Obsolete Media is currently closed to the public as it resides privately in the owner’s home. By reimagining the information architecture of its website, this collection becomes more accessible for a wider range of users with varying levels of media expertise.

Project Duration

9 weeks
(Oct - Nov 2019)

Team Size

Design team of 4
(including me)

My Responsibilities

Content / IA Analysis, Card Sort, Tree Tests, Wireframming

Tools Used

Content Auditor, XMind, OptimalSort, Balsamiq, Photoshop, Treejack

Defining the Problem Space

The Museum of Obsolete Media is currently a content-based digital museum represented by an online exhibit. After a preliminary literature review, we wanted to analyse the website in-depth to find any areas for potential improvement. These were the key insights in our discovery process.

Discovery

Content Inventory

To analyse the website, we initially conducted a content inventory using Content Auditor.

In total, Content Auditor crawled 93 pages. This is a reasonable sample considering the size of the website. Moreover, many pages within the website has similar content, format, and data types. As such, we employed the Noah’s Ark approach.

Among our main findings, we discovered that the average reading grade was 13.4 and the average reading ease was 46.4 per page. According to Flesch-Kincaid readability tests, this is almost double the reading difficulty that is recommended.

Although users may be professionals or hobbyists with experience in obsolete media, new users may find this reading difficulty troubling. Ad-hoc analysis seems to show that this difficulty is distributed across the website relatively equally.

Aside from content difficulty, we also found accessibility, SEO, and loading time issues. For example, there were 1640 broken links, 98% of images had missing alt texts, and 12 pages had missing meta discriptions.

Please visit the official report for more details.

Current IA Schematic Diagram

After our content inventory, we created a preliminary IA schematic diagram to outline the current status of the web museum.

In the following sections, we subsequently analysed the website in greater detail.

Organizational Analysis

In a top-down way, the primary and secondary navigation links within the site are organized based on topical/categorical differences. When navigating within these links, individual artifacts are divided according to chronological differences.

This may prove to be confusing for first-time visitors. Introducing bottom-up links on the front page may help new users understand where to start.

Labeling Analysis

The labelling systems are generally helpful throughout the website, though may sometimes be inconsistent. From a professional user’s perspective, these labels may seem logically indexed and are arguably easy to browse. Unfortunately, a new user or a novice hobbyist may not necessarily feel the same way.

Suggesting similar items may help the user understand these labels with the use of bottom-up architecture; specifically, popular tags, related tags, and similar formats.

Navigation Analysis

The persistence of the navigation bar implies that it is meant to be used to navigate the website at a top-down, global level. However, it does not indicate where the user is at the current moment. This point is further supported by the lack of breadcrumb trails. The site seems to rely heavily on supplementary navigation systems in deeper pages.

Search System Analysis

Unfortunately, the search system does not offer the option to specify particular search zones. This may be argued as a reliance on the browsability of the subject hierarchy.

As such, the search bar may be catered specifically to users who can define the information that they want. For the users that can't specify what they're looking for, the search system can be improved in terms of usability.

Understanding the User

Given that obsolete media is a relatively specific niche, we would expect the digital museum to have different kinds of users. Here, we brainstormed different user groups and analysed their interactions while using the current interface in order to identify areas of potential improvement.

User Analysis

Defining Target Users

Our background research informed our brainstorming sessions where we grouped potential users into four segments according to their level of expertise.

Then, we recruited four participants for each level of expertise using a mix of convenience and snowball sampling.

The following proto-personas embody the average demographics and characteristics for each of the participants in all four expertise levels.

Analysing the Scope

After we developed a better understanding of how our users felt, we needed to specify how we would approach a potential solution. We conducted a card sort study with the previously described proto-personas which informed our proposed revisions and new IA schematic.

Ideation

Card Sort Study

To get a better understanding of how these personas interacted with the digital museum and what we could improve, we conducted a open card sort. We used 30 labels in total, sourced from the current global navigation as well as prominent tags.

Two participants were recruited to represent each proto-persona, for a total of eight responses. The following similarity matrix shows a summary of how different labels were grouped.

After analysing the card sort, we found consistency between the About and the Media Preservation sections, indicating that the participants were able to intuitively group cards that concerned the metadata of the museum and obsolete media in general.

However, we found inconsistency within the specific formats in the website, suggesting that the users had a shallow understanding of the particular types of media. This discrepency was even found amoung the participants with the greatest levels of expertise.

Proposed Revisions

The seven following revisions were proposed in response to the results of the card sort, as well as the observations made in user analysis.

  1. Single point of entry into collection

    Our analysis so far indicates that a more effective interface would consolidate format types into a single button, providing entry to the museum’s primary content.

  2. Labelling system overhaul

    Our proposed changes to the global navigation menu are based on card sort analysis that showed a clear distinction between items in the collection versus more general content.

  3. Collection filter toggles

    By selecting toggles to hide or display certain traits, a user with any level of familiarity can narrow the collection to a more manageable size.

  4. Collection time-range slider

    Separating the temporal metadata from other details is consistent with results from our card sort, in which we observed users distinguishing years from all other cards.

  5. Homepage curated suggestions

    A sequential, curated journey would provide the context needed to dig deeper into the collection, manifesting as a guided tour of the museum for users without expertise.

  6. Search keyword identification

    If a user searches for a keyword that exists as a collection filter, we propose a technical function that would invite users to instead apply that filter to the collection directly.

  7. Content suggestion mechanism

    This mechanism will benefit those seeking to identify an item if their search isn’t perfectly accurate, drawing from users’ anecdotes about how similar many formats can seem.

New IA Schematic

Applying these proposed revisions and user observations, we ideated on a new IA schematic.

Designing the Solution

In order to visualize the proposed revisions and interactions with the new IA schematic, we created wireframe sketches to facilitate agile evaluation. We also developed a clickable wireframe to illustrate and analyse potential storyboards that were unique to each user type.

Prototyping

Wireframe Sketches

According to the new IA schematic and the revisions proposed, we sketched different screens to get a better understanding of a potential interface that would accomodate our changes.

These sketches allowed us to communicate the differences in IA to potential users and stakeholders while gathering evaluative feedback in an agile way.

Lean Evaluation

We showed our sketched wireframes to the participants of the user analysis as they had prior experience of the web museum, explaining the changes that we made and what they would need to do in order to complete certain tasks. We took note of the tasks they were interested in.

We were also happy to find that participants unanimously felt that the infrastructure was accomodating for their demographic, even though their levels of expertise varied.

Overall, users rated the wireframe highly on all seven facets of Peter Morville's honeycomb of UX, especially in terms of findability.

Clickable Storyboards

Moving forward, we brainstormed unique scenarios that would be characteristic of each of our proto-personas based on lean research, and developed a clickable prototype in Balsamiq.

Stanley (the student) wants to explore the museum by viewing whatever the curator features. Since he has limited background knowledge, he wants to see content that could generally spark his interest but doesn't know what he's looking for specifically.

Eddie (the museum enthusiast) wants to learn more about the museum itself. He finds obsolescent media curation fascinating, and wants to understand how it's done. Being an enthusiast, he'll surely have comments and questions to share with the curator.

Debra Turner (the media technician) is wondering why she can't find exact replacements for her company's audio equipment. She can describe the format and the time period it was used, so she checks details regarding its obsolescence. She also wants to know what replaced it in the industry.

Jayce Henrique (the media historian) is somewhat of a curator himself. Seeing the impressive collection of the museum, he wants to contribute his artifacts to a place that can be accessed by the general public. He wants to collaborate with the museum's curator.

Verifying the Efficacy

In order to evaluate our revisions, we conducted a tree test and observational studies based on the tasks in the established storyboards. We intentionally kept the wireframes low-fidelity and facilitated observations openly to understand what the users expected to see in each page.

Evaluation

Tree Test and Observations

We conducted a tree test with three users per proto-persona to evaluate their ability to conceptually understand the steps needed to complete their respective user scenarios.

Using a different set of participants, we also gathered feedback on the clickable prototype. As they attempted to complete each scenario, we answered their general questions regarding the page's content. This open method allowed us to note any information that the user expected to see.

Users enjoyed the featured format page but didn't necessarily feel that specifying the featured format type was necessary or particularly helpful. They just wanted to see a featured artifact, whatever its type may be.

Users found it easy to contact the curator. However, there was minor contention as to whether information regarding media preservation should be in the About tab or should be kept separate.

All participants agreed that relying on a revised search schematic rather than the current navigation system was a substantial improvement. Users with high levels of expertise gave filter suggestions (e.g. brands).

Some users expected to be able to contact the curator directly in the Wanted page. They also wanted to be able to have the option to send an image of the artifact in case they couldn't describe the format accurately.

Reflection

With observations and tree tests, we found evidence to suggest that our proposed revisions made significant improvements to the existing IA structure for users across expertise groups. Along the way, I learned three key things that will extend to my future work:

Specify User Groups as Needed

There is no one size fits all when it comes to something as complicated as a digital museum! There is value in subdividing personas into multiple personas when their experiences are fundamentally different.

Value the Narrative

We tend to forget that we're designing experiences when we're working something as technical as information architecture. As UX practitioners, we have storytelling superpowers! Use them! Don't forget to base design decisions on the user's story.

UI Facilitates IA, Not the Other Way Around

UX is not UI and UI is not IA! Working in UX, we tend to forget that most experiences are felt, not seen. Never rush to finish a visual interface, especially when the underlying infrastructure is flawed. Focus on the basics and flourish from there.

Moving forward with these revisions, we would like to analyse the labelling system in greater depth. In terms of research, bias was possibly introduced through snowball sampling. Clustered sampling would be a better recruitment method but would require greater resources given the scarcity of media obsolescence experts.
